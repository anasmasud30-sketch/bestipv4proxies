Residential vs Datacenter Proxies: Which Should You Use?

They look similar on a pricing page but behave completely differently in the wild. Here's how residential and datacenter proxies really compare — and a one-line rule for choosing.

Compare9 min readUpdated 4 June 2026

"Residential or datacenter?" is the first real decision anyone makes when buying proxies — and the one most people get wrong. Pick the wrong type and you'll either overpay for trust you don't need, or get blocked instantly on a site that hates datacenter IPs. Let's settle it.

The one-line rule

  • Strict target? Use residential. Tolerant target? Use datacenter.
  • Datacenter = fast and cheap; residential = trusted and harder to block.
  • Dedicated IPv4 (a datacenter sub-type) is best for stable, account-based work.
  • Mixing both is normal — and easiest with a provider that offers all types.

What are datacenter proxies?

Datacenter proxies are IPv4 addresses created in commercial data centres — the same facilities that host websites and cloud apps. They're not tied to an internet service provider or a home connection. Because they're generated at scale, they are cheap, fast and abundant. You can buy hundreds in seconds, and you can get dedicated ones for your exclusive use.

The catch: sophisticated websites maintain lists of known datacenter IP ranges. On strict targets — major retailers, sneaker sites, social networks — a datacenter IP can be flagged the moment it connects, before you've done anything. They're brilliant for SEO tools, public APIs and general scraping of tolerant sites, but a poor fit for the toughest destinations.

What are residential proxies?

Residential proxies route through IPv4 addresses that ISPs assigned to real homes. To a website, the traffic is indistinguishable from an ordinary person browsing from their living room. That authenticity is exactly why residential IPs pass strict anti-bot checks that block datacenter ranges.

The trade-offs: residential proxies are more expensive (billed by bandwidth, per GB), and because rotating pools cycle addresses, an individual IP may not stick around. For most users that's a fair price for getting through where datacenter IPs can't.

Don't forget ISP (static residential)

There's a powerful middle ground: ISP proxies, also called static residential. These live in data centres for speed but are registered under real ISPs, so they enjoy residential-level trust and datacenter-level stability. Because they're static IPv4, they're ideal for long sessions and account management. If your task needs a steady, reputable identity, ISP/dedicated IPv4 is often the sweet spot. So which should you choose?

Start from the target, not the price tag:

  • Scraping a strict retailer, social platform or sneaker site? Residential (or mobile for the very hardest).
  • Tracking SEO rankings, hitting public APIs, or scraping tolerant sites at speed? Datacenter or dedicated IPv4.
  • Managing accounts that need a stable identity? ISP / dedicated IPv4.
  • A mix of all of the above? Choose one provider that offers every type.

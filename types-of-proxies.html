Types of Proxies Explained

Residential, datacenter, dedicated IPv4, ISP and mobile — five proxy types, five different jobs. Here's what each one is, where it wins, and where it falls short.

Guide11 min readUpdated 4 June 2026

The biggest factor in whether your proxies succeed isn't the brand on the invoice — it's whether you picked the right type for your target. Each type is sourced differently, priced differently and trusted differently by the websites you reach. Get this match right and even a modest plan performs; get it wrong and the most expensive network in the world will still get you blocked. Here's the full breakdown.

Residential proxies

Residential proxies route your requests through real IP addresses that ISPs have assigned to genuine home devices. To a website, the traffic looks like an ordinary person browsing from their living room — which is exactly why residential IPs sail past the strict anti-bot systems that flag datacenter ranges.

Best for: major retailers, sneaker and ticket sites, social platforms, travel and price aggregation — anything heavily protected. Trade-offs: they're billed by bandwidth and cost more per GB than datacenter, and rotating pools mean individual IPs change frequently.

Datacenter & dedicated IPv4 proxies

Datacenter proxies are IPv4 addresses hosted in data centres rather than on home connections. They are fast, plentiful and cheap, and you can buy dedicated IPs for your exclusive use — no sharing with strangers. This makes them perfect for speed-sensitive, high-volume work on tolerant targets, and for tasks that need a stable, private IP such as SEO tools or specific account workflows.

Best for: SEO and rank tracking, public APIs, general scraping of tolerant sites, and any job where raw speed and low cost matter most. Trade-offs: the strictest websites recognise datacenter ranges and may block them, so they're a poor fit for the toughest targets.

ISP / static residential proxies

ISP proxies (sometimes called static residential) are a clever hybrid: they live in data centres for speed and reliability, but their IPv4 addresses are registered under real ISPs, so websites treat them with residential-level trust. Because they're static, the same IP stays with you for as long as you need it.

Best for: long-lived sessions, account management and any workflow where a steady, reputable IP avoids triggering security checks. Trade-offs: they cost more than plain datacenter IPs and the available pool is smaller than rotating residential.

Mobile (4G/5G) proxies

Mobile proxies use IP addresses from real cellular carriers. Thanks to carrier-grade NAT, thousands of real users share each mobile IP at once, which makes these addresses extraordinarily hard to block — punishing one would punish many legitimate customers. That trust comes at a premium price.

Best for: the most aggressively defended targets, mobile-first platforms and social automation where nothing else gets through. Trade-offs: they're the most expensive type and bandwidth can be more variable than wired connections.

Rotating vs. static: a quick word on rotation

Cutting across all the types above is the question of rotation. Rotating proxies issue a fresh IP per request or on a timer — ideal for large scraping jobs that benefit from spreading load across many addresses. Static (sticky) proxies hold one IP for a session or indefinitely — essential for logins, carts and anything stateful. The best providers let you choose per request, so you can rotate when scraping and stay sticky when managing an account, all from one plan.

How to combine proxy types in one workflow

Experienced teams rarely use a single type for everything — they mix them to balance cost and success. A common pattern looks like this: discover and crawl tolerant pages with cheap datacenter IPv4; switch to residential IPs for the strict pages that block datacenter ranges; hold a static ISP / dedicated IPv4 address for any logged-in or session-based steps; and reserve mobile IPs only for the handful of targets nothing else can crack. Routing each part of a job through the cheapest type that still works can cut your bill dramatically without hurting results.

A note on IPv4 vs IPv6 within each type

Every type above can in principle be offered over IPv4 or IPv6. In 2026 we still recommend leading with IPv4 for anything high-value, because websites and anti-bot systems trust IPv4 ranges more. IPv6 can be cheaper at massive scale, but only performs well on targets that explicitly support it. When in doubt, choose IPv4 — see why IPv4 still matters for the full reasoning.

Which type should you choose?

  • Scraping a strict retailer or social site? Residential (or mobile for the very hardest).
  • Tracking SEO rankings or hitting public APIs at speed? Datacenter or dedicated IPv4.
  • Managing accounts that need a stable identity? ISP / static residential, or dedicated IPv4.
  • Verifying mobile ads or automating mobile apps? Mobile 4G/5G.

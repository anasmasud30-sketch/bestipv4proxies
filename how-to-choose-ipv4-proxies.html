How to Choose IPv4 Proxies in 2026

The proxy market is full of jargon and inflated claims. This guide strips it back to the decisions that actually matter — so you buy the right proxies the first time, at the right price.

Buying Guide14 min readUpdated 4 June 2026

Picking proxies feels harder than it should be. Every provider promises the biggest pool, the fastest speeds and the best price — and they can't all be right. The good news: once you understand five core decisions, choosing becomes simple. This guide walks through each one in order, then shows you how to validate your choice on a trial before committing real money.

Key takeaways

  • Match the proxy type to your target before anything else — it dictates cost and success rate.
  • Compare the true cost per GB (or per IP), not the headline discount.
  • Bigger, fresher pools mean fewer blocks — but the right locations matter more than raw size.
  • Always test on a trial against your real targets before scaling up.
  • Our overall value winner is Cheapest Proxies, which bundles every type in one pay-as-you-go account.

1. Start with the target, not the provider

The single most common mistake is shopping for a provider before understanding the target. Your destination website determines which proxy type will work — get this wrong and no amount of pool size or speed will save you.

Strict targets — major retailers, sneaker sites, social networks, travel and ticketing platforms — run sophisticated anti-bot systems that block datacenter IP ranges on sight. These demand residential or mobile IPs that look like ordinary home users. Tolerant targets — most public APIs, SEO rank tracking, general web data and many search tasks — work perfectly on cheap, fast datacenter or dedicated IPv4 proxies. Identify which camp your target falls into first.

2. Work out the true cost

Headline pricing is designed to look attractive at the largest tier. What matters is what you will pay at your real volume. For bandwidth-billed residential proxies, calculate the effective price per gigabyte at the plan size you'll actually buy. For dedicated work, compare price per IP per month. And read the fine print on expiry — some providers void unused data at the end of the month, while others (including our top pick) let it roll over.

Quick maths: a plan that looks cheap per-GB at 100 GB may be twice the price at the 5 GB you really need. Always price the tier you'll buy, then divide by the gigabytes (or IPs) you'll use.

3. Judge pool size — and location — together

A larger pool of fresh IPs means you repeat addresses less often, which lowers your block rate on rotation-heavy jobs. But raw pool size is a vanity metric if the IPs aren't where you need them. A network with 100M global IPs but thin coverage in your target country may serve you worse than a smaller, well-distributed one. Confirm availability at the granularity you need: country at minimum, and city or carrier for localized work.

4. Decide rotating vs. static (sticky)

Rotating proxies hand you a new IP each request or on a timer — perfect for spreading large scraping jobs across thousands of addresses. Static or sticky proxies keep one IP for a session or indefinitely, which is what you want for account management, checkout flows and anything where a flickering IP would trip security checks. The best providers let you choose per request, so you're not locked into one behaviour.

5. Test before you scale

Vendor averages rarely match your specific use case. Use a free trial, free tier or money-back window to run your real workload — your targets, your request patterns — and measure two numbers: success rate (the share of requests that return valid data) and response time. A provider that benchmarks brilliantly on someone else's targets can still underperform on yours. Spend an hour here and you'll save weeks of frustration.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Chasing the lowest sticker price on a plan far bigger than you need — you'll overpay in practice.
  • Using datacenter IPs on strict targets and blaming the provider when they're blocked.
  • Ignoring expiry rules and losing data you paid for at month-end.
  • Relying on free public proxy lists for anything that matters — they're slow, unreliable and sometimes unsafe.
  • Skipping the trial and discovering the success rate problem only after you've committed budget.

Key questions to ask any provider

Before you hand over a card, get clear answers to these. A good provider will answer all of them plainly; evasiveness on any one is a red flag:

  • How is your residential IP pool sourced? Look for clear, consensual opt-in. Ethical sourcing is more reliable and more defensible.
  • What's the true price at my volume — and does data expire? Confirm the effective per-GB or per-IP cost at your real usage, and whether unused data rolls over.
  • Which proxy types and locations do you offer? Make sure they cover the countries (and cities, if needed) and the type your target demands.
  • How does rotation work, and can I get sticky sessions? You want per-request rotation for crawls and sticky IPs for logins — ideally both, configurable.
  • What authentication do you support? Username/password and IP whitelisting are both useful; pick a provider that offers what fits your setup.
  • Is there a trial or money-back window? You need a risk-free way to validate success rate on your own targets.
  • What support do you offer? Responsive chat or email matters when a job breaks at 2am. (Our top pick offers 24/7 chat and email.)

A quick decision framework

If you only remember one thing, remember the order of operations: target → type → true cost → trial. Identify how strict your target is, pick the proxy type that beats it, calculate what you'll really pay at your volume, then prove it on a trial before scaling. Skipping straight to price comparisons is how people end up with cheap proxies that don't work on the only site they cared about.

What is a proxy server?

A proxy server is an intermediary computer that forwards your web requests on your behalf. Instead of connecting to a website directly, your traffic is routed through the proxy, which presents its own IP address to the destination. The site sees the proxy's location and identity, not yours. When the response comes back, the proxy relays it to you. This simple indirection is the foundation of online privacy tooling, large-scale data collection, ad verification and much more.

Proxies differ from VPNs in scope and design. A VPN typically encrypts all traffic from your device through one provider, prioritising privacy for an individual. Proxies are usually configured per-application or per-request and are built for scale — rotating across thousands or millions of IP addresses so that automated tools can make many requests without being rate-limited or blocked. For data work, account management and geo-testing, proxies are the right tool.

Why IPv4 proxies still matter in 2026

Despite IPv6 being available for years, IPv4 proxies remain the default for serious work, and that's exactly why we put them front and centre. The overwhelming majority of websites, anti-bot vendors, sneaker sites, sportsbooks and social platforms were architected around IPv4. Their fraud and abuse systems have years of reputation data on IPv4 ranges, and they tend to treat unfamiliar IPv6 blocks with extra suspicion — sometimes blocking them outright.

IPv6 proxies are cheaper to provision at huge scale, which is appealing for tolerant targets like certain search and SEO tasks. But for the targets most users actually care about, IPv4 simply works more reliably. The practical 2026 playbook is straightforward: lead with IPv4 (residential, ISP or dedicated datacenter) for high-value targets, and reserve IPv6 for volume tasks on sites that explicitly welcome it. Types of proxies explained

Choosing the right type matters more than chasing the biggest pool. Here are the four categories you'll meet, and when each one wins.

Type 01

Residential proxies

Real IP addresses assigned by ISPs to genuine home devices. They look like ordinary people browsing, so they pass the strictest anti-bot checks. Best for major retailers, social networks, sneaker sites and travel aggregators. They cost more per GB and are usually billed by bandwidth.

Type 02

Datacenter & dedicated IPv4

Fast, cheap IPv4 addresses hosted in data centres. Speeds are excellent and you can buy dedicated IPs for exclusive use — perfect for SEO tools, account management and tolerant targets. The trade-off: strict sites recognise datacenter ranges and may block them.

Type 03

ISP / static residential

Hosted in data centres but registered under real ISPs, so they combine datacenter speed with residential trust. Because they're static IPv4, they're ideal for long sessions and account-based workflows that need a steady, reputable IP.

Type 04

Mobile (4G/5G) proxies

IPs from real mobile carriers. Carrier-grade NAT means thousands of users share each address, making mobile proxies the hardest to block — but also the priciest. Reserve them for the most aggressively protected targets.

How to choose a proxy provider

With the categories clear, the decision comes down to matching a provider to your job, budget and risk tolerance. Run through this checklist before you pay for anything.

Define the target and proxy type

Strict targets (retail, social, sneakers) demand residential or mobile IPs. Tolerant targets (SEO, public APIs) run fine on cheaper datacenter IPv4. Get this right first — it dictates everything else.

Calculate the true cost per GB or per IP

Ignore headline discounts. Work out what you'll actually pay at your real volume. For bandwidth plans compare price per GB; for dedicated work compare price per IP per month. Watch for expiring data.

Check pool size, locations and freshness

A larger, fresher pool means fewer repeated IPs and fewer blocks. Confirm the provider actually has IPs where you need them — country, and ideally city or carrier level.

Test success rate and speed on a trial

Use a free trial or refund window to run your real workload. Measure success rate and response time against your own targets — vendor averages rarely match your specific use case.

Weigh support, billing flexibility and ethics

Look for responsive support, pay-as-you-go billing with no lock-in, and a clear statement on how residential IPs are sourced. These separate a good provider from a frustrating one.

Proxy protocols: HTTP, HTTPS & SOCKS5 explained

Beyond the type of IP, proxies also differ by the protocol they speak. The protocol decides what kind of traffic the proxy can carry and how much it can "see". For most users the choice is simple, but knowing the difference helps you configure tools correctly and avoid head-scratching errors.

Protocol

HTTP

The classic web proxy protocol, built for standard http:// traffic. It understands web requests and can cache and filter them, which makes it efficient for plain web browsing and scraping of non-encrypted endpoints. Today it's almost always paired with HTTPS support.

Protocol

HTTPS

An HTTP proxy that also tunnels encrypted https:// connections. Because virtually the entire modern web is encrypted, HTTPS proxy support is essential — every provider in our top 15 offers it. Your data stays encrypted end-to-end through the tunnel.

Protocol

SOCKS5

A lower-level, protocol-agnostic option that routes any kind of traffic — web, email, P2P, game and more — not just HTTP. SOCKS5 is the most flexible choice for non-web tools and is favoured for performance-sensitive, high-concurrency work.

Which should you pick? For everyday web scraping and browsing, HTTP/HTTPS is perfect and the default in most tools. Reach for SOCKS5 when you need to proxy something that isn't standard web traffic, or when a tool specifically asks for it. The good news: leading providers — including our #1 pick — support all three, so you won't have to switch vendors to change protocol.

Proxy pricing explained: what you'll actually pay

Proxy pricing confuses almost everyone at first, because providers bill in several different ways. Understanding the three common models lets you compare like-for-like and avoid nasty surprises on your invoice.

  • Per gigabyte (bandwidth): the standard model for residential and mobile proxies. You pay for the data you transfer, typically $1.10–$6 per GB in 2026. Cheaper per-GB rates usually require larger upfront commitments — so price the tier you'll really use.
  • Per IP, per month: common for dedicated datacenter and ISP/IPv4 proxies. You rent specific addresses for exclusive use, often under $2 per IP. Ideal when you need a stable, private identity rather than rotation.
  • Per port / unlimited bandwidth: a flat monthly fee for a number of concurrent connections with no data cap (e.g. Geonode, Storm Proxies). Predictable and great for bandwidth-heavy jobs, but you pay whether you use it or not.

Two extra factors quietly change the real price. First, data expiry: some providers void unused gigabytes at month-end, while others (including our top pick) let it roll over. Second, minimum commitments: a low headline rate often hides a high monthly minimum. Always divide your total monthly spend by the data or IPs you'll genuinely use to find your true cost.

Common proxy mistakes to avoid

After buying, testing and occasionally breaking proxies across dozens of projects, we see the same avoidable errors again and again. Sidestep these and you'll save money and frustration:

  • Using datacenter IPs on strict targets. Major retailers and social sites block datacenter ranges instantly. Match the proxy type to the target — residential or mobile for tough sites.
  • Buying the biggest plan for the lowest sticker price. A cheap per-GB rate on a plan far larger than you need means you overpay in practice. Price the tier you'll actually buy.
  • Ignoring data expiry. Losing paid-for gigabytes at month-end is pure waste. Prefer non-expiring or rollover data if your usage is uneven.
  • Relying on free public proxy lists. They're slow, short-lived and sometimes outright malicious. Use a reputable provider's free tier for testing instead.
  • Rotating when you should be sticky (and vice-versa). Rotate for scraping; stay sticky for logins and carts. The wrong mode triggers blocks and broken sessions.
  • Skipping the trial. Always run your real workload on a refund window before scaling. Vendor averages rarely match your specific targets.

Are proxies legal? Ethics & safety

Using a proxy is legal in the vast majority of countries. Proxies are mainstream business tools used every day by analysts, marketers, security teams and developers for privacy, price monitoring, ad verification and collecting publicly available information. What determines legality is what you do with the proxy, not the proxy itself.

To stay on the right side of the line, follow a few principles: only collect data that is publicly accessible; respect each website's terms of service and applicable laws such as GDPR and CCPA where they apply; never use proxies to access accounts you don't own or to bypass paywalls and security you have no right to; and avoid hammering small sites with aggressive request rates. Responsible providers also matter — choose networks that source residential IPs with informed consent and operate transparent, opt-in supply.

A note on sourcing: the residential IPs you rent ultimately belong to real people. Reputable providers obtain them through clear, consensual opt-in programmes and let users leave at any time. We weight transparency of sourcing in our scoring, and we encourage you to favour vendors who publish their policy plainly.

Bottom line: proxies are legitimate, valuable tools when used responsibly. Treat them like any other professional instrument — within the law, within each platform's rules, and with respect for the people whose connections make residential networks possible.

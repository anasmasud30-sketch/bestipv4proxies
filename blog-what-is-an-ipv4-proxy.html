What Is an IPv4 Proxy? A Plain-English Guide

The fundamentals, explained simply: what an IPv4 proxy is, how it works, the main types, and why IPv4 still dominates the proxy world in 2026.

Basics8 min readUpdated 4 June 2026

If you've researched proxies for web scraping, account management or online privacy, you've seen the term "IPv4 proxy" everywhere. But what does it actually mean? In this guide we'll explain it from first principles — no jargon, no assumed knowledge — and show why IPv4 proxies remain the default choice in 2026.

Key takeaways

  • An IPv4 proxy is a middleman server that gives your traffic a 32-bit IPv4 address instead of your own.
  • IPv4 is the older, dominant addressing system — and websites trust it more than IPv6.
  • IPv4 proxies come in residential, datacenter, ISP and mobile flavours.
  • For most real-world tasks in 2026, IPv4 simply works more reliably than IPv6.

First, what is an IP address?

Every device on the internet needs an address so data can find it — just like your home needs a postal address for mail. That's an IP address. IPv4 (Internet Protocol version 4) is the original and still most common format. It looks like four numbers separated by dots, for example 192.0.2.45, and provides about 4.3 billion unique addresses. Because the world ran out of fresh IPv4 addresses years ago, they've become a scarce, valuable resource — which is part of why IPv4 proxies carry real weight with websites.

IPv6 is the newer system, designed to solve that scarcity with a vastly larger address space. It works well, but adoption is uneven, and many websites and anti-bot systems still treat IPv6 ranges with suspicion. We cover this trade-off in depth on our homepage section about why IPv4 proxies still matter.

So what is an IPv4 proxy?

A proxy server is an intermediary computer that sits between your device and the websites you visit. Instead of connecting directly, your request goes to the proxy, which forwards it on using its own IP address. The destination site sees the proxy's IPv4 address — not yours — and sends the response back through the proxy to you.

An IPv4 proxy, then, is simply a proxy that presents an IPv4 address to the websites you reach. That single substitution unlocks a lot: you can appear to browse from a different city or country, distribute thousands of requests across many addresses so you aren't rate-limited, keep your real identity private, and run multiple accounts that look like separate users.

How an IPv4 proxy works, step by step

  1. You configure your browser, app or script to route traffic through the proxy's address and port.
  2. Your request travels to the proxy server first, rather than straight to the target website.
  3. The proxy forwards the request to the target using its own IPv4 address.
  4. The target replies to the proxy, believing the proxy is the visitor.
  5. The proxy relays that response back to you. The website never sees your real IP.

The main types of IPv4 proxy

"IPv4 proxy" describes the address format, but proxies are also categorised by where the IP comes from. The four you'll meet are:

  • Residential — real IPv4 addresses from home ISPs; the most trusted, ideal for strict targets.
  • Datacenter — fast, cheap IPv4 hosted in data centres; great for tolerant targets and bulk speed.
  • ISP / static residential — datacenter-hosted but registered to real ISPs, blending speed with trust.
  • Mobile — IPv4 from 4G/5G carriers; the hardest to block, but the priciest.

IPv4 vs IPv6 proxies: the short version

IPv6 proxies are cheaper to produce in huge quantities, which is attractive for very high-volume tasks on sites that support IPv6. But the majority of websites, anti-bot vendors and social platforms were built around IPv4 and have years of reputation data on those ranges. The practical result in 2026: IPv4 proxies pass more checks on more targets. Lead with IPv4 for anything high-value, and reserve IPv6 for tolerant, large-scale jobs.

The bottom line

An IPv4 proxy is a server that lends your traffic a trusted IPv4 address, letting you browse, collect data and manage accounts as if from somewhere — or someone — else.
